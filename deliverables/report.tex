\section{Introduction}

We study the paper \textit{StelLA: Subspace Learning in Low-rank Adaptation using Stiefel Manifold}~\cite{stella}, which proposes a geometry-aware extension of LoRA~\cite{lora} for parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT). Where LoRA updates a frozen weight matrix $W \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ via a two-factor low-rank decomposition $W + BA^\top$, StelLA replaces this with a three-factor form $W + \frac{\alpha}{r} U S V^\top$, constraining $U \in \mathrm{St}(r,m)$ and $V \in \mathrm{St}(r,n)$ to lie on Stiefel manifolds throughout training. The diagonal matrix $S \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times r}$ captures magnitudes while $U$, $V$ learn orthonormal subspace bases via Riemannian optimization (gradient projection + polar retraction~\cite{absil}).

The paper makes two central claims: (1)~this geometric constraint enables more effective subspace learning than standard LoRA, consistently improving downstream accuracy (up to $+1.3$ points on commonsense reasoning, $+0.25$ on image classification vs.\ DoRA~\cite{dora}); and (2)~the overhead is small, adding only $r^2$ parameters per layer. We design a series of controlled experiments to verify claim~(1) at small scale, using functional expressivity as a proxy for representational capacity.

\section{Background and Relation to Course}

\paragraph{Theoretical intuition.} SVD-based initialization~\cite{pissa} improves LoRA but only guides the start of training. StelLA maintains orthogonality \emph{throughout} by constraining $U, V$ to $\mathrm{St}(r, m) = \{Y \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times r} \mid Y^\top Y = I_r\}$. Euclidean gradients are projected to Riemannian gradients ($\mathrm{grad}_Y = \nabla_Y - Y(\nabla_Y)^\top Y$), then retracted to the manifold after each optimizer step. This cleanly separates subspace direction ($U$, $V$) from magnitude ($S$), analogous to the weight-decomposition in DoRA~\cite{dora}.

\paragraph{Connection to class.} StelLA sits at the intersection of several course topics. As a PEFT method (Lecture~5), it reduces trainable parameters from $O(mn)$ to $O((m+n)r + r^2)$, adapting a frozen foundation model to downstream tasks. Its implementation modifies only the optimizer step of the training loop (Lecture~1), adding gradient projection and SVD retraction, while leaving the forward and backward passes untouched. This illustrates how algorithmic innovations can target a single phase of training. Finally, the Riemannian optimization extends the Euclidean gradient descent framework from Lecture~1 to constrained, non-Euclidean parameter spaces.

\section{Experimental Setup}

We use our \texttt{expressivity} library~\cite{expressivity}, which we previously developed, to compare the \emph{functional expressivity} of LoRA and StelLA adapters. Given two architecture families $\mathcal{A}$ and $\mathcal{B}$, this framework samples a random function $a \sim \mathcal{A}$, generates targets $y = a(x)$ on a fixed input batch, and trains $b \in \mathcal{B}$ to minimize $\|b(x) - y\|^2$. The resulting MSE captures how well $\mathcal{B}$ can approximate $\mathcal{A}$'s function space, independently of any particular dataset or downstream task---isolating the architectural contribution from confounding factors.

Our base architecture is a single-layer decoder-only Transformer ($d{=}8$, 2 heads) built on nanoGPT~\cite{nanogpt} blocks, as studied in class. We apply LoRA and StelLA adapters (official Sony implementation~\cite{stella_code} via HuggingFace PEFT~\cite{peft}) to the attention and MLP projections at ranks $r \in \{1, \ldots, 5\}$, and include a full (unconstrained) Transformer as a neutral baseline. Each comparison runs 1000 random trials per rank with 5 gradient steps (lr${}=0.01$, batch size 256). We report mean and minimum fitting loss. All experiments run on a laptop CPU without GPU.

We consider three comparisons: (a)~\emph{Direct:} LoRA fits StelLA outputs and vice-versa; (b)~\emph{Baseline:} both fit a full Transformer; (c)~\emph{Ablation:} StelLA vs.\ an \emph{Euclidean three-factor} variant---identical $USV^\top$ parameterization (via StellaConfig) but trained with standard AdamW, without Riemannian hooks. This isolates the effect of the geometric constraint from the three-factor decomposition itself.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Direct Comparison}

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{figures/direct.png}
    \caption{Direct comparison: mean fitting loss with min--mean shaded region (1000 trials per rank).}
    \label{fig:direct}
\end{figure}

StelLA consistently achieves lower fitting loss than LoRA across all ranks (Figure~\ref{fig:direct}): $-21\%$ at $r{=}1$ ($9.05 \times 10^{-4}$ vs.\ $1.15 \times 10^{-3}$), narrowing to $-10\%$ at $r{=}5$. In best-case, StelLA at $r{=}3$ already matches LoRA at $r{=}5$. The shaded regions reveal lower variance for StelLA (mean-to-min spread of 31\% vs.\ 42\% at $r{=}1$), suggesting the manifold constraint regularizes the optimization landscape.

\subsection{Baseline Comparison}

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{figures/baseline.png}
    \caption{Baseline comparison: both methods approximate a full Transformer. Shaded region shows min--mean spread.}
    \label{fig:baseline}
\end{figure}

When both methods approximate a full Transformer (Figure~\ref{fig:baseline}), LoRA is better at $r{=}1$ ($-12\%$), but StelLA crosses over at $r \geq 4$ and achieves the lowest loss at $r{=}5$ (mean: $7.74 \times 10^{-4}$ vs.\ $7.83 \times 10^{-4}$; min: $4.76 \times 10^{-4}$ vs.\ $4.93 \times 10^{-4}$). Notably, StelLA's loss curve is strictly decreasing while LoRA exhibits a non-monotonicity at $r{=}3$, consistent with a more stable optimization landscape under the Stiefel constraint.

\subsection{Ablation: Stiefel vs.\ Euclidean Geometry}

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{figures/ablation.png}
    \caption{Ablation: Euclidean 3-factor vs.\ StelLA (same $USV^\top$, different optimizer).}
    \label{fig:ablation}
\end{figure}

The Euclidean and Stiefel variants produce remarkably similar expressivity curves (Figure~\ref{fig:ablation}), with StelLA marginally better at every rank ($<1\%$ gap at $r{=}5$). This is consistent with the paper's own ablation (Table~5 in~\cite{stella}), where the Euclidean variant is competitive. A key limitation of our protocol is the short optimization horizon (5 gradient steps per trial): the Stiefel constraint has little time to differentiate itself.

To assess StelLA's computational overhead---directly relevant to large-scale deployment---we benchmark GPU memory, throughput, and per-step time on a single NVIDIA T4 GPU, using the same Transformer architecture at ranks $r \in \{8, 32, 64, 128\}$.

\paragraph{Memory.} Figure~\ref{fig:mem_phases} breaks down memory by training phase. Both LoRA and StelLA show identical peak memory after the forward pass, dominated by activations. The key difference appears in the \emph{before forward} phase: StelLA stores fewer optimizer states than LoRA since only the diagonal $S$ requires full Adam states, while $U$ and $V$ use simpler Riemannian updates. At $r{=}128$, StelLA reduces peak memory by $15.5\%$ relative to LoRA ($1988.6$ vs.\ $2353.1$\,MB, Figure~\ref{fig:peak_mem}). At low ranks ($r{=}8$), both methods use nearly identical memory (${\approx}1660$\,MB), $28\%$ less than the full Transformer ($2309.2$\,MB).

\paragraph{Throughput.} StelLA's Riemannian optimizer step (gradient projection + polar retraction via SVD) introduces a per-step cost that grows with rank (Figure~\ref{fig:throughput}). At $r{=}8$, the throughput gap is modest ($30$ vs.\ $32$ samples/s, $-6\%$), but at $r{=}128$ it widens to $-28\%$ ($18$ vs.\ $25$ samples/s). LoRA remains faster than the full Transformer at all ranks, while StelLA drops below the Transformer baseline at $r{=}64$.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{figures/benchmark_time_breakdown.png}
    \caption{Per-step time breakdown (forward / backward / optimizer) in milliseconds.}
    \label{fig:time_breakdown}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Time breakdown.} Figure~\ref{fig:time_breakdown} decomposes the per-step wall time into forward, backward, and optimizer phases. Forward and backward times are nearly identical between LoRA and StelLA at each rank, confirming that the Stiefel constraint does not alter the computational graph of these phases. The overhead is entirely concentrated in the optimizer step: at $r{=}128$, StelLA's optimizer phase takes ${\approx}140$\,ms vs.\ ${\approx}10$\,ms for LoRA, accounting for the throughput gap observed above. This is expected, as the polar retraction requires an SVD of an $r \times r$ matrix at each adapted layer per step. At low ranks this cost is negligible, but it scales cubically with $r$, making it the dominant bottleneck at $r{=}128$. This throughput penalty is the main practical cost of the Stiefel constraint and should be weighed against the expressivity and memory gains reported above.

\section{Limitations and Discussion}

Several caveats apply to our findings. Our models are single-layer Transformers with $d{=}8$, far from the multi-layer, high-dimensional architectures (LLaMA-7B, ViT-Large) on which StelLA reports its strongest gains; the observed trends may not transfer directly to larger scales. The expressivity framework measures functional capacity rather than downstream task accuracy, and uses only 5 optimization steps per trial---too few for the geometric constraint to fully manifest its advantage, as evidenced by the narrow ablation gap. Finally, LoRA's advantage at $r{=}1$ in the baseline setting suggests the Stiefel constraint may be overly restrictive when the rank budget is very small.

Despite these limitations, the experiments provide consistent evidence that StelLA's function space is at least as expressive as LoRA's and more stable across initializations, even in a minimal setting. The computational benchmarks reveal a concrete trade-off: StelLA saves up to $15\%$ peak GPU memory thanks to lighter optimizer states, but incurs a throughput penalty of up to $28\%$ at high ranks due to its Riemannian optimizer step---a trade-off that practitioners should consider when choosing between the two methods.
